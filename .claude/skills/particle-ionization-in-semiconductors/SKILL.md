# Particle Ionization in Semiconductors

Analyze carrier generation yield and lattice damage severity when high-energy particles interact with semiconductor materials.

## When to Use
- Semiconductor is exposed to X-rays, fast electrons, or nuclear particles (protons, α-particles)
- Designing or analyzing solid-state detectors
- Evaluating dosimeters
- Assessing radiation damage effects

## Analysis Procedure

### 1. Identify Particle Type
Determine which type of incident particle is involved:
- X-ray photons
- Fast electrons  
- Nuclear particles (protons, α-particles, etc.)

### 2. Determine Excitation Location

**Bulk Excitation:**
- Particles: X-rays or γ-rays
- Characteristic: Penetrate deeply into material

**Near-Surface Excitation:**
- Particles: Fast electrons or α-particles
- Characteristic: Excitation concentrated near surface

### 3. Analyze Carrier Generation Process

- **Cascade Character**: Initially generated fast carriers create secondary carriers of sequentially lower energy
- **Carrier Yield**: A large number of carriers are produced per incident particle
- **Generation Mechanism**: Ionization creates electron-hole pairs throughout excitation volume

### 4. Assess Lattice Damage

Evaluate damage severity based on particle properties:

| Particle Type | Damage Severity | Damage Mechanism |
|---------------|----------------|------------------|
| X-rays | Low to moderate | Photochemical reactions |
| Fast electrons | Moderate | Atomic displacement |
| Protons, α-particles | High to severe | Impact damage, cascades |

**Damage Dependence:**
- Increases with particle energy
- Increases with particle mass

## Output
- Carrier generation yield assessment
- Lattice damage severity classification
- Excitation profile (bulk vs near-surface)
- Recommendations for detector design or damage mitigation
