# Theoretical Background for Deep Level Formation

## Green's Function Formalism

The deep level formation theory is derived from Green's function calculations using semi-empirical tight-binding Hamiltonians. This approach allows for accurate prediction of defect energy levels within the band gap.

## Vacancy States: A1 and T2

### A1 State
- Spherically symmetric (s-like) state
- Forms from a1-symmetric combination of dangling bonds
- Critical for determining s-orbital impurity interactions

### T2 State
- Triply degenerate (p-like) state
- Forms from t2-symmetric combination of dangling bonds
- Critical for determining p-orbital impurity interactions

## Bonding and Antibonding State Formation

When an impurity atom is inserted into a vacant site:

1. **s-orbital interaction**: Interacts primarily with A1 vacancy state
2. **p-orbital interaction**: Interacts primarily with T2 vacancy state

Each interaction produces:
- **Bonding state**: Lower energy, typically merges with valence band
- **Antibonding state**: Higher energy, may appear in gap or conduction band

## Transition Analysis

### Deep Well Regime
- Large |V0| values
- Deep level center with vacancy-like charge density
- Strong localization around defect site
- Energy levels relatively insensitive to potential variations

### Intermediate Regime
- Moderate V0 values
- Transition from vacancy-like to hydrogen-like character
- Charge density begins to spread

### Shallow Level Regime
- Small V0 values (near conduction band intersection)
- Effective mass approximation becomes valid
- Hydrogen-like wavefunction spread
- Energy levels follow hydrogenic series

## Spectrum Compression Effect

The two-band influence causes spectrum compression:

$$\left|\frac{\partial E}{\partial V}\right| \rightarrow 0 \text{ as } |V_0| \rightarrow \infty$$

This means:
- Deeper potentials lead to energy levels that are less sensitive to the exact potential depth
- Vacancy states represent the limiting case for deep levels
- Shallow levels are more sensitive to potential variations

## Practical Implications

1. **Donor-like states**: Form when impurity has extra electrons compared to host
2. **Acceptor-like states**: Form when impurity has fewer electrons compared to host
3. **Vacancy states**: Represent the ultimate limit for both donor and acceptor character
