# Geminate Recombination Analysis

Determine when and how geminate recombination occurs in materials with short mean free paths and Coulomb-attractive centers.

## When to Use
- Materials with short mean free path (organic crystals, amorphous semiconductors)
- Excitation from Coulomb-attractive centers
- Analyzing recombination dominated by localized centers
- Studying effects of additional IR excitation on recombination

## Geminate Recombination Process

### 1. Excitation Process
Carrier is excited from a Coulomb-attractive center higher up into the band.

### 2. Relaxation Process
Carrier undergoes multiple scattering processes until it is within kT of the band edge.

### 3. Geminate Condition Check

**IF** the carrier reaches the band edge **WITHIN** the same Coulomb funnel of the original center:

**THEN** it recombines with the **SAME** center from which it was generated.

**ELSE** (out-diffusion): Carrier escapes the Coulomb funnel and recombines elsewhere.

## Key Factors

- **Mean Free Path**: Determines likelihood of staying within Coulomb funnel
- **Coulomb Funnel Size**: Spatial region of influence of the defect center
- **Scattering Rate**: Affects how quickly carrier reaches band edge

## Material Contexts
Geminate recombination is:
- Commonly observed in **organic crystals**
- Plays important role in **amorphous semiconductors**
- Also applies to **geminate band-to-band recombination**

## Out-Diffusion Interruption

An additional IR excitation can:
- Increase probability of carrier escape from Coulomb funnel
- Reduce geminate recombination rate
- Enable alternative recombination pathways

## Output
- Determination of whether geminate recombination or out-diffusion occurs
- Assessment of factors affecting recombination probability
- Recommendations for controlling recombination pathways
