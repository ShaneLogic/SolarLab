A visual walkthrough of the simulator

What the solver computes

SolarLab solves three coupled fields — electrons n, holes p, and mobile ions P — self-consistently with electrostatics across a thin-film stack, to steady state or through a bias sweep. Each section isolates one part of the formulation: the governing equations, the spatial and temporal discretisation, the contact and initial conditions, and the ETL/absorber and absorber/HTL heterojunction physics that governs perovskite device behaviour.

SEC 01

Three fields, one coupled system

The state vector is y = (n, p, P) per grid node. Two carrier-continuity equations transport charge, the ionic species obeys a Nernst–Planck drift-diffusion equation in the same field, and Poisson’s equation relates the electrostatic potential φ to the net space charge. There is no fourth unknown: φ is obtained from the current (n,p,P) by a direct solve once per right-hand-side evaluation, not time-integrated.

— electron continuity —
∂n/∂t = (1/q)·∂xJn + G − R

— hole continuity —
∂p/∂t = −(1/q)·∂xJp + G − R

— drift-diffusion fluxes (Scharfetter–Gummel) —
Jn = q·Dn(∂xn − n·∂xφ/VT)
Jp = −q·Dp(∂xp + p·∂xφ/VT)

— ion drift-diffusion (Nernst–Planck) —
∂P/∂t = ∂x[ DI(∂xP + P·∂xφ/VT) ]

— Poisson (solved, not integrated) —
−∂x(ε ∂xφ) = q(p − n + P − NA + ND)

The equations are bidirectionally coupled: a perturbation to the ionic profile changes the space charge, hence the potential, hence the carrier fluxes, which feed back into the space charge. This mutual dependence is the origin of the numerical stiffness and of the hysteresis seen in perovskite J–V measurements.

in the code solver/mol.py:assemble_rhs builds ∂t(n,p,P); physics/poisson.py does the tridiagonal φ solve each call. # state y=(n,p,P), φ derived — never part of y
SEC 02

Generation — where the carriers come from

The generation term G in the continuity equations is set by optical absorption. Under AM1.5G illumination, each absorbed above-gap photon generates one electron–hole pair; the resulting position-dependent rate is the optical generation profile G(x), the source that drives photocurrent.

Two optical models are available. Beer–Lambert gives exponential attenuation, G(x) = α·Φ·e−αx, concentrating generation near the illuminated face. The transfer-matrix method (TMM) resolves coherent thin-film optics — forward and backward waves in every layer — so back-contact reflection produces a standing-wave interference pattern in G(x). The two models are selectable in the figure.

SEC 03

Recombination — where the carriers die

The loss term R accounts for every carrier pair that recombines before collection. Three bulk mechanisms act in parallel; their relative magnitude depends on the injection level (carrier density).

Shockley–Read–Hall recombination (trap-assisted, ∝ n) dominates at low injection. Radiative band-to-band recombination (∝ n², rate B·(np−ni²), the detailed-balance inverse of generation) dominates at intermediate density. Auger recombination (three-body, ∝ n²p) dominates at high injection. Open-circuit voltage is set by the condition that total recombination balances total generation. Interface recombination is a distinct surface mechanism, treated in section 12.

in the codephysics/recombination.py (SRH n₁/p₁, radiative, Auger) · DeviceStack.het_recomb_despike. # radiative uses net B·(np−ni²) — detailed balance at equilibrium
SEC 04

Space — a tanh-clustered multilayer mesh

Under the method of lines, space is discretised first. The solution varies most rapidly in the thin depletion regions at the heterojunctions, so a uniform mesh would under-resolve the interfaces while over-resolving the quasi-neutral bulk. SolarLab applies tanh node-clustering within each layer and concatenates the per-layer meshes into a single non-uniform grid.

Nodes concentrate at the layer boundaries and thin out through the bulk. Each layer carries its own node count, so an interface is never bracketed by two widely spaced nodes — a single over-wide cell there would smear the band offset.

in the code discretization builds the tanh mesh; jv_sweep._layer_node_counts sizes each layer. # graded layers get grading_N_mult extra refinement
SEC 05

The flux — Scharfetter–Gummel & the Bernoulli function

When the potential drop across a cell exceeds the thermal voltage, the carrier density varies exponentially between adjacent nodes, and a central-difference flux becomes inaccurate and unstable. The Scharfetter–Gummel flux is the exact solution of the constant-field drift-diffusion equation across a single cell:

Jn = (qDn/Δx) · [ B(−Δφ/VT)·nR − B(+Δφ/VT)·nL ]
Bernoulli  B(x) = x / (ex − 1)  →  B(0)=1 (pure diffusion),  large x → upwind drift

At Δφ = 0 the Bernoulli weights are symmetric and the flux reduces to central-difference diffusion. As the field increases, the weighting shifts continuously onto the upwind node and the scheme becomes drift-dominated, with no change of formula and no loss of stability. This continuous upwinding is what keeps the discretisation stable across the 103× density contrasts at a heterojunction.

in the code discretization/fe_operators.py (Bernoulli-weighted flux) → physics/continuity.py:carrier_continuity_rhs. # harmonic-mean face D; same form for J_p with sign flipped
SEC 06

Time — implicit Radau over a stiff system

Spatial discretisation reduces the PDE system to a large set of coupled ODEs — one per field per node, the "lines" of the method of lines. The system is stiff: the dielectric-relaxation and ionic time constants span many orders of magnitude. An explicit integrator would be step-size limited by the fastest mode; SolarLab uses Radau IIA, an L-stable implicit Runge–Kutta method that damps the fast modes and takes large stable steps toward steady state.

The figure relaxes an illuminated state toward its steady-state operating point. The implicit integrator converges monotonically; the overlaid explicit trajectory shows the oscillation an explicit method incurs near the stiff regime, which motivates the choice of an implicit method.

SEC 07

Method of Lines — grid to the global system

Sections 04 and 06 treated the spatial and temporal discretisations separately; the method of lines combines them. Space is represented by the tanh-clustered nodes x0…xN. Each node carries three unknowns — n, p, P — and stacking them over all nodes forms the global state vector y of length 3(N+1): the full discrete representation of the device.

The spatial operators — the Scharfetter–Gummel flux and the Poisson coupling — reach only a node's immediate neighbours, so the Jacobian ∂F/∂y is block-tridiagonal: each node's rows couple only to nodes i−1, i, i+1. This banded structure gives an O(N) linear solve. Time integration is a single evolution equation over the whole vector, dy/dt = F(y). Each node's trajectory in time is one "line" — the origin of the method's name.

in the codepacked state y = (n,p,P) per node · solver/mol.py assembles F(y) · solve_ivp(Radau) marches it. # block-tridiagonal Poisson factored once (dgttrf)
SEC 08

The solve, step by step

Both drivers — a Radau stage in the transient integrator and the direct steady-state solver — reduce to the same Newton–Raphson iteration: from a trial state, evaluate the residual (the equation imbalance), solve a linearised system for the correction, apply a damped update, and repeat until the residual falls below tolerance.

— one Newton iteration —
1 · residual    F(yk) = ∂t(n,p,P)  (= 0 at steady state)
2 · Jacobian   J = ∂F/∂y  (finite-difference, LU-factored once, reused across chords)
3 · linear solve  J·Δy = −F  (tridiagonal sweep — the second figure)
4 · line search  yk+1 = yk + λ·Δy  (damped, in log-density space)
5 · test       ‖F‖ < tol ?  stop : repeat

The early iterations are damped (λ<1): a full Newton step is not taken while the residual is large. Once the iterate is within the region of convergence, the method enters its quadratic regime and the residual drops by orders of magnitude per iteration. The state is accepted once the residual crosses the tolerance.

The linear solve J·Δy = −F exploits the banded structure. The Poisson operator is tridiagonal, so it is factored and solved by the Thomas algorithm (LAPACK dgttrf/dgttrs): a forward elimination of the sub-diagonal, then a back-substitution that fills in the correction Δφ. Applying the correction updates the profile; the residual is eliminated over a small number of sweeps.

in the code experiments/steady_state.solve_steady_state (damped log-space Newton, LU reuse) · physics/poisson.py:factor_poisson / solve_poisson_prefactored (dgttrf/dgttrs). # residual scaled by peak density; dual convergence criteria + J-error-bounded stall accept
SEC 09

The coupling loop

The full system is nonlinear and self-referential. The four equations form a closed dependency cycle: the carrier and ion densities determine the space charge; the charge determines the potential through Poisson; the potential drives the fluxes; the fluxes update the densities, which change the charge. No single pass satisfies all four simultaneously, so the system is iterated to a self-consistent fixed point.

Each traversal of the cycle is one evaluation of assemble_rhs: Poisson re-solved from the current densities, fluxes recomputed, the state advanced; the self-consistency residual decreases with each pass. Both the transient integrator (Radau) and the steady-state Newton solver drive the cycle to its fixed point. Newton couples all four blocks in a single Jacobian, converging in few iterations rather than the many required by a decoupled (Gummel-type) sweep.

in the codeone lap = one solver/mol.assemble_rhs call (Poisson solve → SG fluxes → ∂t). # Radau iterates laps per step; steady-state Newton couples all blocks in J
SEC 10

Boundaries — how the contacts are held

The contact boundary conditions specify both the potential and the carrier densities at the terminals. The potential is fixed by Dirichlet conditions φ(0)=0 and φ(L)=Vbi−Vapp; applied bias lowers the right-contact potential and reduces the built-in field.

Two carrier-boundary models are provided. The default is an ideal-ohmic Dirichlet condition: terminal densities are held at their dark-equilibrium values. The Robin (selective-contact) model instead imposes a finite surface-recombination flux J = ±qS(density − densityeq), recovering a blocking contact as S→0 and the ohmic limit as S→∞.

Two built-in potentials with different sign conventions coexist. The configured stack.V_bi is a positive magnitude (the IonMonger free-parameter convention) used by the default boundary. compute_V_bi() returns the signed work-function difference φ(right)−φ(left): positive for the conventional p-contact-left stack, and negative for an n-contact-left device (CIGS, n⁺/p silicon). Consumers requiring a magnitude (sweep range, Voc bracket) take its absolute value; the flat-band contact model uses the signed value directly.

in the code solver/mol.py — V_bi_bc = compute_V_bi() if flat_band else stack.V_bi; Robin flux in physics/contacts.py. # φ(L)=V_bi_bc−V_app at every Poisson BC site
SEC 11

The starting state — equilibrium seed & ionic memory

Each run is initialised from a physically consistent state rather than zero. A dark run is seeded from solve_equilibrium and settled to the true zero-current state; an illuminated run is seeded from the illuminated steady state. The initial state is significant because the mobile-ion distribution retains memory of the prior bias history.

In a J–V sweep, each voltage step is initialised from the previous step's settled state. Because the ionic response is slow relative to the scan rate, the forward and reverse scans reach each bias with different ionic configurations, and hysteresis emerges directly from the physics rather than being imposed. The ion profile lags the applied bias, while the electronic subsystem responds essentially instantaneously.

in the code experiments/jv_sweep.run_jv_sweep reuses the prior steady state as IC → ionic memory preserved. # degradation freezes ions (D_ion=0 copy) for each snapshot
SEC 12

Heterojunctions — the ETL/absorber & absorber/HTL interfaces

At each heterointerface the electron affinity χ and band gap Eg are discontinuous, producing conduction- and valence-band offsets. Three distinct mechanisms act at this discontinuity, each treated explicitly; the conduction-band-offset control ΔEC varies its magnitude.

1 · Thermionic-emission cap

Past a 0.05 eV offset the SG flux would over-emit across one cell. The flux is capped to the Richardson–Dushman thermionic limit at that face.

2 · Effective-DOS QFL step

Boltzmann transport requires a VT·ln NC term in the band potential. Omitting it introduces a spurious kT·ln(DOS-ratio) quasi-Fermi-level step — a Voc offset.

3 · Interface SRH

Cross-carrier recombination on the interface trap, referenced to detailed balance, using densities projected onto the junction plane.

+ · TFE tunnelling

Optional Padovani–Stratton enhancement lets carriers tunnel through a sharp spike, lifting the thermionic cap toward the SG ceiling.

in the code fe_operators.thermionic_emission_flux · DeviceStack.dos_band_potentials · physics/interface_plane.py. # TE faces where |ΔE_C| or |ΔE_V| > 0.05 eV are precomputed in MaterialArrays
SEC 13

The architecture — from YAML to a J–V curve

The data flow is unidirectional. A YAML preset (or the UI) constructs an immutable DeviceStack; this is compiled once into a MaterialArrays cache — all per-node and per-face arrays plus a pre-factored Poisson operator; an experiment driver then evaluates assemble_rhs against that cache many times and assembles the result.

The central design distinction is build-once versus per-RHS. Most physics is folded into the cache at build time — static, evaluated once. A small number of state-dependent hooks (radiative reabsorption, field-dependent mobility, Robin contacts) deliberately break the build-once invariant and recompute on every evaluation. This distinction organises much of the code structure.

A single right-hand side serves two drivers: the transient Radau integrator (full dynamics, mobile ions, hysteresis) and the ion-free steady-state Newton solver (the SCAPS-comparison operating point). Three tiers — LEGACY ⊆ FAST ⊆ FULL — act as a physics ceiling: a flag activates only when the configuration supplies the required parameters.

LEGACY

All optional physics disabled — bit-exact IonMonger reproduction; the regression baseline.

FAST

All build-once upgrades on (TE, TMM, ions, trap profile, photon recycling); per-RHS hooks off.

FULL

Everything, including the three per-RHS hooks (reabsorption, μ(E), Robin).

Two drivers, one RHS

Transient Radau for dynamics; steady-state Newton for the parity operating point.
